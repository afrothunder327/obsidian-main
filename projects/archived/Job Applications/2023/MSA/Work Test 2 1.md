# Preparing for a Pandemic: Accelerating Vaccine Availability
## Executive Summary
Governments can improve the likelihood and timeliness of pandemic-time vaccine availability by financing manufacturing capacity for a broad portfolio of vaccine candidates in parallel with their development.
## Summary
Delays in vaccine rollout during pandemics have enormous social and economic costs, but are likely to occur due to market and regulatory forces.[^4] Social and political limits on acceptable vaccine prices mean that the social value of vaccines far exceeds their private returns.[^1] Furthermore, . In light of these inefficiencies, Ahuja et al. provides a succinct discussion of how governments can most effectively accelerate pandemic-time vaccine availability.

**Key Point 1: Support a diverse vaccine portfolio to increase the odds of vaccine availability.**
Developing vaccines for novel pandemic pathogens involves working at the scientific frontier where vaccine effectiveness and safety are uncertain. Governments should therefore support a broad and diversified portfolio of vaccine candidates and technologies that reduce the total and correlated risk of failure.[^3] A diverse portfolio of candidates that succeed also reduces the risk of shortages and monopolies that incentivize vaccine nationalism.

**Key Point 2: Use push finance to incentivize parallelized vaccine development and manufacturing capacity installation.**
Vaccine developers typically delay investments in manufacturing capacity until vaccine candidates' technical and regulatory uncertainty are resolved. To ensure timely vaccine availability, governments can de-risk firm investments in manufacturing capacity made in parallel with vaccine development. To do so, governments can either:
1. Directly compensate firms for upfront investments in manufacturing capacity and commit to purchasing the vaccines at marginal cost (push financing), or
2. Commit to purchasing a given supply of vaccines at a rate that justifies the firm's manufacturing investments given its belief its vaccine R&D will succeed (pull financing). 

Based on a stylized model of vaccine contracting under incomplete information, Ahuja et al. recommends a financing approach primarily based on push funding, as it is likely to lead to lower government expense. If a government wants many firms to attempt vaccine development but cannot distinguish between their likelihood of success, pull financing may result in large payouts to incentivize firms with lower success probability. At the same time, governments will not want to  spend on manufacturing capacity for firms that are very unlikely to succeed, and will want to incentivize speed in vaccine development, too. Some financing should therefore also be allocated via pull funding.

**Key Point 3: Invest in substantial manufacturing capacity, and do so early.**
If vaccine supply is bottlenecked by manufacturing capacity, prices will skyrocket and shortages may ensue. During COVID-19 pandemic, firms were with government support able to expand capacity more quickly than many expected, but even greater capacity would have allowed the world to complete vaccination one year earlier than it did. Timely expansions in manufacturing capacity can be incentivized using pull financing as discussed in point 2, and can be further eased by subsidizing the supply of critical materials like bioreactors, delivery devices, and adjuvants so they are available when needed.


Delays in vaccine rollout during pandemics have enormous social and economic costs. Before vaccines were made available, each month of the COVID-19 pandemic caused 300,000 deaths and a $500 billion decline in global GDP.[^2]

[^1]: Castillo et al. (2021) estimates that increasing vaccine capacity from 2 to 3 billion doses per year would have generated $2.5 trillion in social value, while resulting in only $30 billion in additional firm revenue at $15 per dose.
