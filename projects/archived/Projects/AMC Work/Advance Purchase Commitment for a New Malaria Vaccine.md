### Introduction

### Background on AMCs
- Even if malaria only sped up one year by AMC, cost would be $80-90 per life saved.
- Are we able to calibrate the returns that we expect different disease indications to return? If we can show that we are well calibrated on this then we can be sure that we aren't giving out exorbitant rents.
- "From a dynamic standpoint rewards to individual developers should ideally reflect the marginal value of each vaccine to society; while it is difficult to precisely measure that marginal value, a system should aim to generate at least a rough correspondence between innovation incentives and the value of the innovation. Once a qualifying vaccine is developed, the incremental benefit of subsequent vaccines will typically be smaller, suggesting that in most cases it will typically be appropriate to provide smaller rewards for subsequent vaccines than for the initial vaccine, particularly if the subsequent vaccine is developed significantly later (in which case a large portion of R&D expenditures for the subsequent entrant would have been incurred after the successful launch of the first product was clear)."
	- That's pretty interesting.
- US Orphan Drug Act -- 1983 law provided seven years of exclusive market rights for drugs developed to treat muscular dystrophy, Huntington's, and ALS, since they had a small market size of 200,000. Seven year exclusivity could be pre-empted by "clinically superior" drugs.
- 280 drugs approved under this act; only three were second market entrants.
- "In expectation, increases in commitment size will be competed away through additional entry while more entry will on average increase the probability that a workable vaccine is developed and increase the speed with which it is produced."
	- So the trade-off is between cost and likelihood of success. How should we think about this trade-off. You can pick the choice that maximizes expected value or something.

### Estimating AMC size
- They basically use an opportunity cost approach to estimate how much is needed to get pharma companies to work on this.
- They get a revenue NPV of $3.44 billion.
- The revenue data that they use reflects existing products and therefore includes "low-hanging fruit" that may have been cheaper to develop.
- They estimate how much larger the commitment size needs to be to account for the fact that you may get multiple firm entry. They base it on how many secondary product have been created for vaccines over the last thirty years; they get 1.69, so adjust AMC size up by 69%. I get this in principle but should also do some analysis on what is welfare maximizing. For this one needs a distribution over firm probabilities of success.

### Cost-effectiveness of the AMC
- It seems like they focus in on specific target populations. You should maybe start by pulling out those target populations as well.
- About $15 per DALY saved
- Wow more than 300 million people were contracting Malaria every year. I don't think HCV is more than a few million.
- You should read the piece about a malaria vaccine. How much would an AMC have sped it up?
- You should also discount program costs apparently, although I am unsure about this.
- They malaria vaccine would be delivered as part of a standard vaccination program. Need to think about this part for HCV. What are costs if no in this standard package?
- They assume linear adoption over seven years until it reaches steady state
- Their calculations do not consider epidemiological herd benefits.
- They also don't model targeting the vaccine; just assume it will be given randomly to people.
- They don't talk about underlying treatment rates.

### Speedup times
- "While only ten new orphan drugs were discovered in the decade prior to the Orphan Drug Act, 200 were discovered in the next decade (Grabowski, 2003)."
	- That's pretty useful information.
- "A vaccine commitment is also likely to substantially accelerate access in the poorest countries since the sponsor’s price is paid on a per-person immunized basis. In contrast, when the hepatitis B vaccine was introduced at $30 per dose, it was rarely used in low-income countries"
- They say the historical record points to delays of 10 to 15 years, but they don't really provide much evidence. Also, how are you supposed to know where on that timeline you find yourself as a funder?
