# Green Ammonia

## Overview of Green Ammonia Technologies ([Medium](https://medium.com/extantia-capital/technology-overview-four-pathways-to-produce-green-ammonia-and-auxiliary-technologies-for-an-361fb6721a5e))
> While ammonia production currently emits around 500 Mt CO2e annually, the [Net Zero in 2050 scenario would only allow 17 Mt CO2e](https://www.iea.org/reports/ammonia-technology-roadmap/executive-summary) to be emitted by ammonia production annually. All in all: there’s a lot of work to do.

The four decarbonization technologies outlined are:
1. **Haber-Bosch with green hydrogen**,
2. **Direct electrolysis**,
3. **photocatalytic**, and 
4. **biological pathways**.

A fifth is improving the efficiency of ammonia usage, much of which is lost during fertilizer spread and re-releasing soil reactions.
### Haber-Bosch with green hydrogen
Challenges:
1. The energy efficiency of water electrolysis to produce green hydrogen has to improve. 
2. Secondly, the general electrolyser costs have to come down.
3. Thirdly, the costs of cheap renewable energy are the main cost driver of green hydrogen and should be as low as possible.
>In the best-case scenario, it will likely take [at least until 2035 for green hydrogen to reach price parity with grey hydrogen](https://pubs.acs.org/doi/10.1021/acsenergylett.2c01615). That said, government support schemes, such as subsidies for green hydrogen and carbon pricing could significantly speed up this process.

There are three different electrolyser methods for green hydrogen: **alkaline electrolyser**, **PEM**, and **solid oxide**.

>Regarding the costs involved in green hydrogen production: the costs are initially (in 2020) correlated with TRL according to [alkaline < PEM < solid oxide](https://pubs.acs.org/doi/10.1021/acsenergylett.2c01615). By 2050, however, that order is predicted to change to [solid oxide < alkaline < PEM](https://pubs.acs.org/doi/10.1021/acsenergylett.2c01615).

> With carbon taxes, the production prices for [green ammonia (in USD per kg) are predicted as follows in 2050](https://pubs.acs.org/doi/10.1021/acsenergylett.2c01615): solid oxide: 0.35 < alkaline: 0.42 < PEM: 0.60. These predictions were, however, made with lab-scale data assuming high CAPEX and OPEX and no economies of scale.
- So presumably pessimistic.

You should spend some time looking into solid oxide electrolysers, as there may be AMC potential there.

### Direct electrolysis
Aims to create ammonia directly from nitrogen and water in the air.
The technology is far from technologically ready.

### Photocatalysis
Uses solar energy and semiconductor to transform nitrogen and water into ammonia.
> In essence, [water is oxidised by photogenerated holes and nitrogen is reduced by photogenerated electrons](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2451929421000383) yielding ammonia and oxygen as reaction products.

> Although the commercial use of photocatalysis is currently limited to a small number of processes, breakthroughs in reactors, as well as catalyst design, could potentially change that. For example, [light-driven catalysts were capable of cracking ammonia into hydrogen at the same efficiency as thermal catalysts](https://newatlas.com/energy/light-catalyst-ammonia-rice/). This result could be promising for photocatalytic green ammonia production too. Another study found that a [combination of reactor design and the use of CO2 as a scavenging agent significantly improved the NH3 formation](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1385894722025220#b0255) rate. Even though these results were obtained at a lab-scale, they could be meaningful at a commercial scale too given the right financial as well as engineering support.

Seems more promising.

The thing about ammonia is that it's a really simple compound, so I doubt that a demand demonstration is as important as in other industries. It's very obviously a one-for-one replacement.

### Biocatalysis
Leveraging enzymes that can produce ammonia in the way that they do in soils to make nitrogen bio available for plants. This would be done in a biorector.

Downside is that energy costs of a bioreactor are high, perhaps 10x of the Haber-Bosch process.
### Overview of companies
![Overview  of green ammonia companies](https://miro.medium.com/v2/resize:fit:828/format:webp/1*RiXEOHWXbqOnu0T2okZbrw.png)

>About 80% of Haber-Bosch ammonia is used as agricultural fertiliser. In 2005, approximately [100 Mt NH3 from the Haber–Bosch process was used in global agriculture, whereas only 17 Mt NH3 were eventually consumed by humans](https://wedocs.unep.org/bitstream/handle/20.500.11822/8212/Reactive_Nitrogen.pdf?sequence=3&isAllowed=y) in crop, dairy, and meat products.

So there is a big opportunity on the loss and waste side if one can figure out solutions.

## The Royal Society Report on Green Ammonia
[Link](https://royalsociety.org/-/media/policy/projects/green-ammonia/green-ammonia-policy-briefing.pdf)
- Ammonia production generates 1.8% of total carbon dioxide emissions
- The two primary decarbonization options are CCS or using hydrogen generated from renewable-powered electrolysis. This would be to decarbonize the cracking of hydrogen from water.
- There is quite a bit of discussion of how ammonia can be used as an alternative fuel especially for shipping. There is a chart showing how much lower the shipping costs are. [[The HC Insider Podcast Conversations in Energy  Commodities - Trading Green Ammonia with Christophe Savi|This podcast]] also discusses that it's apparently not that difficult to capture or catalyse the N20 that is produced when using ammonia as a fuel, which could effectively make it green (although presumably adds costs).
