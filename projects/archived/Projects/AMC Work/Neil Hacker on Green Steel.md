### Steel production technologies
Here's a quick overview from Neil Hacker's page.
![Overview](https://www.scalingcarbonremoval.com/img/steel/steeltech.png)

![Emissions comparison of different methods](https://www.scalingcarbonremoval.com/img/steel/emissions.png)
Note that the above chart doesn't include Molten oxygen electrolysis or direct reduced iron with hydrogen.
#### Blast Furnace-Basic Oxygen Furnace (BF-BOF)
This is the dominant steel production method (73% of global production).
![BF-BOF|400](https://www.eurofer.eu/assets/Uploads/overview-of-the-bof-process-poster-1030x1200.jpg)
The method basically works by reducing iron oxide using carbon, typically in the form of coal. This is why the process generates greenhouse gas emissions. 

Emissions intensity of **~2.3tCO2e / ton steel**.
#### Electric arc furnace
This method recycles used steel and produces 22% of the world's steel.
![EAF| 600](https://www.eurofer.eu/assets/Uploads/overview-of-the-eaf-process-poster-eurofer-blue-1094x603.jpg)
It seems like ou also mix some new iron ore, so the quality grade on this is probably lower.

Emissions intensity of **~≤0.3tCO₂ per ton of steel**.
#### CCUS on BF-BOF
This involves installing carbon capture technology on blast furnaces and finding downstream storage or utilization for the captured carbon. Note that this can't eliminate all of the CO2 emissions from the process (not sure of the technical reasons why, but would be good to look into). Neil Hacker says for the US the potential reduction is ~50% on a BF-BOF plant, while SSAB indicates it could be between 60-70% ([link](https://www.ssab.com/en/brands-and-products/docol/automotive-steel-resources/automotive-insights/comparing-green-steel-technologies)).

The IEA predicts that CCS will only cover 1% of steel production by 2030.

According to SSAB, "Some emissions experts state that CCS reservoirs should be prioritized for industries other than steel – such as plastics or cement – that face large costs and obstacles in developing fossil-free technologies. In these sectors, the carbon dioxide could be reduced by up to 60–70%." ([link](https://www.ssab.com/en/brands-and-products/docol/automotive-steel-resources/automotive-insights/comparing-green-steel-technologies))

A downside of CCS is that it generates a persistent green premium. This is fine if you have strong regulation, but also leaves potential long-term cost reductions on the table.

Emissions intensity of **0.9 t CO2e per ton of steel**.
#### Direct reduced iron (DRI) powered by NatGas or H2

There are several different ways you can use hydrogen to decarbonize steel production, but the approach with the most climate potential is reducing iron oxide using hydrogen generated using renewable energy.  This can bring the greenhouse gas emissions from iron reduction to virtually zero, although there are still more emissions from other parts of the process (but reduction really dominates).

According to Neil Hacker these plants can first be powered using natural gas and later transitioned to hydrogen. Using natural gas would cut emissions by ~90%, so is still significant, but this obviously requires its own rather expensive capital buildout.

Note that hydrogen production is extremely energy intensive and we need to generate a lot more energy to make this viable.
