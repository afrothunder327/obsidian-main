### Introduction

Recent years have seen tremendous progress in the deployment of low-carbon technologies in the energy and and transportation sectors. Solar and wind energy capacity grew 36% and 22.6%, respectively, between 1990 and 2021, implying a respective doubling in capacity every 2.25 and 3.4 years. For solar, in particular, most of that growth has come since 2010. Similarly, for electric vehicles, sales have grown 58% annually since 2010, growing from almost 0% to 18% of total car sales between 2010 and 2023.

These developments represent big wins for overall climate change mitigation. Solar, wind, and electric vehicles are now cost-saving technologies in many geographies, meaning we should anticipate continued rapid growth in total deployment. This is a big deal for climate change mitigation. As the figure from Our World in Data below illustrates, electricity, heat and transport produce the most greenhouse gas emissions out of all economic sectors. While we can hardly claim victory before we truly reach net zero, these technologies represent concrete pathways to deep and lasting emissions reductions.

<iframe src="https://ourworldindata.org/grapher/ghg-emissions-by-sector" loading="lazy" style="width: 100%; height: 600px; border: 0px none; text-align:center"></iframe>

Still, one might notice in the chart above that there are many other sectors contributing a large share of greenhouse gas emissions. Indeed, emissions from electricity, heat and transport made up 45% of total greenhouse gas emissions in 2020, while the agriculture, industrial, manufacturing and construction, and building sector make up another 37.5%. We further recognize the size of the work yet to be done when we consider that electricity is only 20% of total energy consumption^[[Source](https://www.iea.org/energy-system/electricity)], and road passenger transport, where electric vehicles are most promising, are only 45% of total transport emissions.^[[Source](https://ourworldindata.org/co2-emissions-from-transport)] Clearly, we have to quickly decarbonize many more sectors to have any chance at reaching net zero emissions by 2050.

Achieving net zero emissions by 2050 will involve decarbonizing emissions from key industries such as steel, cement, ammonia, and protein production, which, to date, have proven difficult and expensive to decarbonize. However, promising technological innovations offer alternate production processes that are markedly cleaner and can become affordable with further process improvements and scale. To supply these green materials at scale, massive new infrastructure investments are required.

Investing in large infrastructure projects for new technologies is often a risky proposition due to the lack of a proven market, and investment is therefore prone to fall short of desired amounts. In clean energy, this problem was solved through **power purchase agreements**, which secured long-term revenue for renewables projects so that investors had guarantees on returns. However, offtake agreements may not be attainable in all of the abovementioned sectors, which tend to rely on spot markets. This begs the question: **If long-term offtakes are not available, how can clean technology project developers demonstrate minimal market risk to risk-averse investors?**

This post outlines an advance market commitment (AMC) mechanism designed to solve this problem. We call it a **demand demonstration AMC**. In a demand demonstration AMC, a clean technology that is a drop-in replacement for an emissions intensive commodity is temporarily subsidized down to the commodity price to prove that buyers will willingly purchase the product once it reaches price parity with the existing commodity. This demonstrates to investors that there is a market on par with size of the commodity market once the technology reaches price parity, therefore eliminating the market risk of infrastructure projects for the technology.

In the sections that follow, we describe the market failure inhibiting investment in clean technology infrastructure in greater detail, outline the demand demonstration AMC mechanism, and describe how the model might be used in several key sectors for decarbonization.

Hard to decarbonize sectors tend to be material/infrastructure heavy. This means they have a couple of properties:
- High capital/fixed costs required to build out infrastructure for low carbon solutions to be implemented at scale.
- Low margins, so investment is not an highly attractive business proposition.

These two conditions mean that private investors need to have large amounts of capital and be willing to tolerate low margins. Institutional lenders/investors such as banks and sovereign wealth funds, who generally seek stable, long-term returns for investments, fit this bill.

However, these investors' tolerance for low margins comes at the expense of risk tolerance. They tend to be very risk-averse, wanting high degrees of confidence that the expected returns from a project will bear out. These conditions are a challenge for emerging clean technologies, which bear both higher **technical risk** and **market risk** than traditional assets/projects these investors hold.

Improving the risk profile of clean technology investments has been a key part of the story for more mature clean technologies. In the energy sector, renewables projects have often been backed by long-term **power-purchasing agreements (PPAs)**, which guarantee a buyer for the renewables generated over 10-30 years. This drastically mitigates the market risk of the investments, and has decreased the total project cost by as much as **XX%** by making the project eligible for cheaper capital.

Unfortunately, many of the abovementioned sectors tend to conduct transaction using **spot markets**, as opposed to through long-term contracting. There is little culture for long-term offtakes, and many buyers are unwilling to take on the risk of long-term purchasing commitments when their needs could change and broader market prices can vary. This begs the question: **If long-term offtakes are not available, how can clean technology project developers demonstrate minimal market risk to risk-averse investors?**
