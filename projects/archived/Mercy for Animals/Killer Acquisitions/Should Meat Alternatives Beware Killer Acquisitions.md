### Introduction
On November 18, 2021, JBS, the world's largest protein company, finalized a $100 million (check) agreement to acquire a controlling stake in Spanish cultivated meat startup BioTech Foods (CITE). According to JBS global CEO Gilberto Tomazoni, the purchase was made to "strengthen" the company's strategy of innovation and "to accelerate the development of the cultivated protein market" (CITE).

I had mixed reactions to reading this news. On the surface, the purchase struck me as a clear signal that large meat companies recognize cultivated meat as a serious market opportunity. On the other hand, I had some instinctive concerns about what the acquisition of animal product alternative (APA) startups could mean for the development of truly competitive alternatives to conventional animal products.

This post addresses one potential risk associated with incumbents acquisition of APAs: killer acquisitions. A killer acquisition occurs when an incumbent firm acquires an "innovative target and terminates the development of the target’s innovations to preempt future competition" (CITE PAPER). In this post, I will briefly describe the recent research on killer acquisitions and try to assess its relevance to the development of APAs. In doing so, I also outline some weak guidelines to assessing the likelihood of an acquisition being intended to terminate a product, and ways we can decrease their likelihood. As a byproduct, I also describe a number of potential risks that are not captured by current models of killer acquisitions, but that I believe potentially could be more important than the risk posed by killer acquisitions.

### Introducing killer acquisitions
Cunningham, Ederer and Ma's *Killer Acquisitions* (2021) (henceforth CEM) provides, I think, the most up-to-date theoretical model and empirical evidence on killer acquisition (CITE). I will try to describe the paper's model and supporting empirical evidence without delving into any technical details.

The paper begins by modelling a scenario in which an incumbent firm faces a decision to purchase a company that is developing a new product that, if successfully developed, will compete with a product in the incumbent's portfolio. If the new product "overlaps" with a product in the incumbent's portfolio, the incumbent's incentive for developing this product is weaker than the innovativing firm's, as the new product would eat into the incumbent's existing profits. In CEM's model, this disincentive can become so strong that the incumbent is motivated to acquire the innovative target merely to shut down the development of the new product. Crucially, incumbents can still possess motive to acquire a competing innovation for the sake of killing it even when the competing innovation is "qualitatively superior to the incumbents' existing projects or products [and] when incumbents benefit from development synergies relative to the entrepreneur" (CITE). In an extension of the model, the authors also show that killer acquisition incentives persist when there are multiple potential acquirers.

According to the model, incumbent firms have stronger incentives to "acquire to kill" when:
1. There is greater "overlap" in the market of the incumbent's product and the competing innovative product.
2. The present and future markets faced by incumbents are more concentrated, as this means each incumbent controls a greater share of the market's total profits, and therefore face greater losses from new competition.

Both of these predictions about killer acquisition incentives are supported by the authors empirical exploration of acquisitions of pharmaceutical startups. First, they find that acquisitions are four times more likely when the acquirer's drug overlaps with the innovating firm's, and that "projects acquired by an incumbent with an overlapping drug are 23.4% less likely to continue development activity as compared to drugs acquired by nonoverlapping incumbents" (CITE) Second, they find that the "decrease in development probability for acquired overlapping projects is concentrated in markets with low competition" (CITE). These findings are supported by a number of robustness checks. (FOOTNOTE TO EXPLAIN THEM).

Additionally, CEM also presents evidence that pharmaceutical killer acquisitions are more likely to occur when they avoid triggering the requirements for a Federal Trade Commission pre-merger review. In particular, overlapping acquisitions showed a tendency to fall right under the review requirements, whereas non-overlapping acquisitions showed no such pattern. Additionally, the acquisitions falling under the criteria were less likely to launch. #### Reasons for concern about killer acquisitions for APAs

Many of the model features and predictions were supported by relatively strong empirical evidence. As such, it seems reasonable to be concerned about killer acquisitions in animal product industries to the extent that they display features that heighten the likelihood of killer acquisitions.

According to the model, market concentration increases the risk of killer acquisitions, and my sense is that animal product processing is highly concentrated in many countries where alternatives development is taking place. For instance, in the US, the top four beef, pork and poultry processing firms hold 82%, 66% and 54% of total market share, respectively (CITE WHITEHOUSE). In Germany, five companies control two-thirds of processed pork ([link](https://eu.boell.org/en/2021/09/07/companies-dominating-market-farm-display-case)) (ADD FOOTNOTE THAT YOU DIDN'T FIND AS MANY STATS AS YOU WANTED). This gives strong incentives for large incumbents to snuff out potential competition through acquisition.

While APAs do not yet seem to be strong substitutes to their counterparts for most consumers (ADD MILK FOONOTE), the clear aim for many APAs is to become like-for-like substitutes, and they seem to be making progress (CITE). As such, I believe they display high degrees of overlap with incumbent animal products, which aggravates killer acquisition risk.

Finally, it seems to me that the risk of killer acquisitions severely impairing the development of APAs is strongest for cultivated meats. Cultivated meats are in earlier stages of development and are more scientifically novel, making their startup costs higher. The high startup costs and uncertainty about their market viability make acquisitions more appealing to cultivated meat entrepreneurs. Finally, acquisitions by large protein incumbents can be an attractive proposition for entrepreneurs seeking to further develop their product, as the incumbents can provide expertise in science, logistics, sales, distribution, and operations scaling that in theory synergize with the improved development of the product.

For similar reasons, CEM's findings from the pharmaceutical industry may not generalize well to APAs. There are a far greater number of ailments than there are animal products categories, and drugs for these ailments are not generally substitutable across ailments. Both these factors contribute to 'siloing' pharmaceutical startups within markets that are strongly differentiated by the drug's target ailment and mechanism. In contrast, there are only perhaps a few dozen animal product categories in which APAs are to compete, and products are more substitutable across categories. This raises the effective numbers of innovative targets an animal product incumbent is concerned about, potentially decreasing killer acquisition incentives for reasons stated above.

Looking at the investment by incumbents into APA companies, there have not been a lot of acquisitions of controlling shares in APA companies to date. In fact, it seems that a lot of investments made by animal product companies into APAs have taken the form of partnerships or acquisitions of minority shares. Perhaps companies can be controlled from a minority shareholder position, but overall this reads to me as if killer acquisitions are not an immediate concern for APAs.

#### Risks beyond killer acquisitions
While killer acquisitions may not be the largest risk for APAs, I think we should still be cautious of acquisitions of APAs by animal product incumbents, as I think they another disincentive in developing APAs that compete with conventional animal products.

The CEM model takes the degree of competitiveness between the entrepreneurial product and the incumbent product as fixed in both the acquisition and non-acquisition cases. I think this could be a significant limitation of the model, as I speculate that incumbents will have a significant incentive to steer the direct of innovation of the innovative product such that it does not compete strongly with its original product. Indeed, I think similar incentives are precisely the reason that we saw animal product incumbents develop low-quality plant-based alternatives for decades until outside entrants like Beyond Meat, Impossible Foods, and others began threatening to compete with their incumbent products. I also want to note that this argument extends beyond just considering acquisitions - any situation in which conventional animal product incumbents are also leading the development alternatives faces this risk, regardless of whether the alternatives were acquired or not.

For this reason, I think it could be crucial that for any given animal product category, there are a number of companies not engaged in the conventional animal product industry that are aiming to develop a product that displaces conventional animal products. Although I haven't modelled these dynamics, I expect that this will put pressure on incumbents to develop products that are strong substitutes for their conventional animal products.

## Appendix

#### How can we spot killer acquisitions?
Even if one does not find the likelihood of killer acquisitions a cause for concern, it may still be useful to know some heuristics that can be used to spot acquisition that are more likely to result in the termination of APAs. As noted above, incumbents have greater incentives to terminate innovative targets when their markets are more concentrated, and when the targets will strongly compete with them. The latter quality, however, is in many cases also likely to be correlated with potential product development synergies, making it challenging to judge whether a product is acquired due to a desire to kill it or due to a unique capacity to further develop it.

In general, I think we should be more concerned about deals for controlling stakes when:
- they occur in more concentrated markets
- there are less potential innovative targets to acquire
- there is greater product overlap between the incumbent and nnovative target
- the deal does not meet pre-merger review requirements.
The original entrepreneurs leaving the development team seems to be another signal that a product may have been acquired to terminate its development. 

#### If killer acquisitions are a risk, how can we respond?
Alternative protein entrepreneurs aiming to displace animal agriculture for more than profit-related reasons should exercise caution in sanctioning potential acquisitions, despite the fact that they also present significant potential upsides. In general, it seems less risky to only sanction the acquisition of minority shares by competing animal product companies. They can use some of the indicators of killer acquisitions as a guide to thinking about the likelihood of their project being killed if acquired.

Further, although I have not yet modelled this, I suspect that promoting the entrance of a greater number of promising APA ventures for any given APA category will decrease the rewards incumbents firms anticipate if they were to acquire a firm to kill it. Thus, stimulatng the entrance of numerous promising APA startups for each animal product cateory could decrease the risk of killer acquisitions.
